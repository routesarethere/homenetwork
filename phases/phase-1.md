# Phase 1 — Wiring the House

[← Back to README](../README.md)

---

## Setup (Late 2021)

I bought my first home at the end of 2021. Once papers were signed and keys in hand, the first thing I did was head off to the Spectrum store to get a modem and my service activated — 300 Mbps down, 20 Mbps up.

90% of my experience is with Juniper hardware, so that was my first go-to. I had a Juniper SRX210 and a Ubiquiti AC-Lite that sat on the living room floor and gave us basic access as we started out. That setup carried us from move-in all the way through early 2022.

Shortly after moving in, I was already planning out where I'd be running ethernet and coax. I wanted wired connections for various devices as well as strong wireless in both the house and the garage. The house was built in the 1940s with a newer detached garage. Landscaping between the two would prevent me from simply burying cable. As the project went on, I figured out my best option would be a wireless point-to-point link between the house and garage, with a network rack and switch in each location — that way I could provide PoE to the access points and future security cameras.

---

## Phase 1a — Feb 2022

I ordered cabling (4 × 250ft Cat6), keystones, patch panels, a crimper, and a wire stripper from Monoprice, plus a Klein cable tester from Amazon. At this point I was feeling pretty good. I've cut and crimped hundreds of cables, I have the networking knowledge, and I now have the materials.

Getting cabling from the basement to the 1st floor was a breeze, as expected. Getting cable to the second story required going all the way up to the attic and dropping down from there — and this is where things got difficult. I lacked the experience fishing cable through old walls and a tight attic.

I put this project on pause and bought a 1984 Honda Goldwing to fix up.

---

## Phase 1b — Dec 2022

Getting fed up with no wifi and poor cellular service in the garage made working on the Goldwing and other projects quite cumbersome — I rely on internet connectivity to research constantly. That frustration was the push I needed to get back on the network project.

With a little PVC conduit and a few hours of sweat, I had all my drops installed in both the house and the garage. I mounted a small 4u rack in the garage to hold a patch panel and switch. In the house, I still just had the patch panel and all the equipment sitting on a card table — but the cable was run, and that felt like progress.
